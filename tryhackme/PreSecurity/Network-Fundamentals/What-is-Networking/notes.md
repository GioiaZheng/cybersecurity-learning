# What is Networking? — Notes

Networking refers to the idea of **connecting multiple devices so they can exchange information**.  
Although the word is technical, the concept exists everywhere in daily life.

---

## 1. Networking in Everyday Life

Before talking about computers, we can observe networks all around us:

- **Transportation networks** — buses, trains, and stations connected in routes.
- **Electrical power grids** — power stations connected to households and cities.
- **Postal systems** — letters move through connected sorting facilities.
- **Social networks** — people connected by relationships, interests, or communication.

All of these work because individual “nodes” are connected and can send something (data, energy, goods, messages) to one another.

Computing networks follow the *exact same idea*, but instead of people or cities, the “things” connected are:

- phones  
- laptops  
- routers  
- servers  
- IoT devices  

---

## 2. What Makes a Computer Network?

A **computer network** is formed when **two or more devices** are able to communicate with each other.  
This communication may involve:

- sending messages  
- sharing files  
- accessing services  
- transferring data  

Networks can be extremely small (two devices) or extremely large (billions of connected systems across the globe).

### Why devices communicate
Devices on a network exchange information for many purposes:

- your phone syncing to cloud storage  
- your laptop accessing a website  
- a smart camera sending video footage  
- a server delivering emails  

The moment communication begins, you’re participating in a network.

---

## 3. Networking Is Everywhere in Modern Life

Networking underpins almost all technology today. Examples:

###  Electricity
Power grids use networking to coordinate energy distribution.

###  Cloud services
Apps like Google Drive or iCloud rely on constant communication between your device and remote servers.

###  Smart infrastructure
Traffic lights, sensors, and surveillance cameras form automated networks.

###  Weather systems
Global sensors upload real-time environmental data to centralised networks.

Because networking is deeply integrated into modern infrastructure, understanding it is essential for cybersecurity.

---

## 4. A Simple Network Example (Alice, Bob, Jim)

TryHackMe uses a simple analogy:

- **Alice**
- **Bob**
- **Jim**

If they exchange messages, they form a network.  
This illustrates that networks are not just big systems — even three connected devices qualify.

Key ideas demonstrated:

- nodes (the people/devices)
- communication paths (arrows)
- data moving between nodes

The structure may be simple, but the principle scales up to the entire Internet.

---

## 5. Networks Come in Many Forms

Computer networks can vary dramatically in:

### ● Size  
From a few devices in your home to millions across continents.

### ● Purpose  
Some networks share files, others stream video, others control traffic signals.

### ● Ownership  
Some networks are *private* (home WiFi).  
Others are *public* and connect the world (the Internet).

### ● Technology  
Wired (Ethernet), wireless (WiFi), fibre-optic, cellular (4G/5G), satellite, and more.

Understanding these differences becomes crucial later when learning:

- LAN & WAN  
- routing  
- IP addressing  
- switching  
- security controls  

---

## 6. Why Networking Matters in Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity heavily depends on networking because:

### ✔ Attacks travel *over networks*  
Malicious traffic, malware propagation, credential theft — all rely on network communication.

### ✔ Defenders monitor *network traffic*  
Security tools detect suspicious patterns in packets and logs.

### ✔ Understanding networks = understanding how attacks work  
You cannot secure a system if you don’t know:

- how devices identify themselves  
- how data travels between them  
- where vulnerabilities may appear

This is why TryHackMe teaches networking early — it is the foundation for understanding both offensive and defensive techniques.

## 7. Key Concepts Learned in This Section

- A **network** is any set of devices connected together.
- Networking happens everywhere, not just in computers.
- Computer communication enables nearly all modern technology.
- Even a small three-person communication example can represent a network.
- Networks differ in size, purpose, and structure.
- Understanding networking is essential for cybersecurity work.

Networking is the backbone of the digital world.  
Without networks:

- there would be no Internet  
- no online services  
- no social media  
- no cloud computing  
- no remote work  
- no cybersecurity  

Everything begins with devices being able to communicate.
